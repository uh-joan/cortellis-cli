LLM Council Verdict

Landscape Skill Production Readiness — April 4, 2026

Question

Is the cli_anything/cortellis/skills/landscape/ skill complete enough for production use? Are there meaningful gaps?

Council Verdict Not Production-Ready

The architecture is coherent, the modes are well-designed, and the recipe structure is sane. But the skill produces confident-looking reports with invisible truncation and ~45% mechanism annotation failure. For the #1 pharma intel use case feeding M&A and licensing decisions, that combination is a liability.

Ship it as a beta with explicit limitations surfaced in the output, or do not ship it as a flagship.

Advisor Alignment

Unanimous Agreement

  • 300-drug cap = silent truncation in oncology
  • ~45% mechanism enrichment failure rate
  • Hardcoded pharma list is a maintenance trap
  • No confidence/coverage signals in output

Key Disagreement

  • Expansionist: gaps are feature seeds, not blockers
  • Rest of council: that's motivated reasoning
  • Tension: future vision vs. current production risk
  • Verdict: production readiness question ≠ roadmap question

Gaps Identified (by severity)

  • Silent truncation at 300 drugs/phase. Oncology has 500+ programs. The report looks complete but isn't. The "missing drug detection" may also be paginated, making the diff circular.
  • No confidence/coverage signals in report output. Users cannot distinguish incomplete data from clean competitive signals. No resolution method warnings, no fill-rate footer, no coverage percentage.
  • Mechanism enrichment half-fails. 40-50% of drugs have no mechanism annotation. 20-lookup cap per phase is too low for large indications.
  • Hardcoded major pharma list. ~20 companies, will go stale with M&A activity. Drives company rankings that feed strategic conclusions.
  • Company scoring rewards activity, not capability. Phase-weighted scoring measures program count. A single blockbuster Phase 3 asset should outweigh ten discovery programs but doesn't.
  • Biosimilar detection is name-based text matching. Misses brand-name biosimilars, may false-positive on "follow-on" descriptions.
  • "No data" vs "no activity" indistinguishable. Empty API returns could mean empty space or poor data coverage. No low-confidence flagging.
  • Deals capped at 20. Deal flow is the connective tissue between pipeline position and commercial validation. 20 is a floor for active indications.
  • ASCII-only output format. No JSON/CSV export alongside the report. Pharma analysts need to paste into decks and models.

The One Thing to Do First

Add a report footer to every generated landscape showing: total drugs fetched vs. API total, mechanism fill rate for that run, entity resolution method used, and a one-line coverage warning if any metric falls below threshold. This single change makes every gap visible to the decision-maker.

Advisor Responses

Contrarian "Confident-looking reports with systematic blind spots — worse than no skill at all"

The fatal flaw is the 300-drug hard ceiling. Six pages of 50 is an arbitrary architectural limit masquerading as a feature. For major indications like oncology or diabetes, there are hundreds of active programs. You will silently truncate competitive landscapes.

The "missing drug detection" mechanism is circular. It fetches ALL drugs without phase filter and diffs against phase CSVs — but if that unfiltered fetch is also paginated, you're comparing two incomplete datasets.

Mechanism enrichment filling "50-60% of empty mechanisms" means 40-50% have no mechanism annotation. That's a core function that half-fails. The company scoring uses a hardcoded list that will go stale. The biosimilar detection is text-matching that will miss brand-name biosimilars.

First Principles "Does this skill tell users what it doesn't know? No. That's the production gap."

The question presupposes "completeness" is the right lens. The real question is: what decisions break if this skill is wrong, and does the skill surface that risk?

Three structural problems: (1) The 300-drug ceiling is invisible to the user. (2) Company scoring rewards activity, not capability — a single Roche Phase 3 asset outweighs ten Discovery-stage startups but the algorithm doesn't know that. (3) The skill cannot distinguish "no data" from "no activity."

Expansionist "Not missing completeness — missing ambition in how it frames what it has"

The long tail of discovery compounds is where you spot emerging mechanisms before competitors do. The major pharma list is the seed of a company-tier taxonomy (tier 1-4). The 40-50% unknown mechanisms cluster around novel programs — surface that as competitive intelligence.

The deals section (up to 20) is criminally underused. Cross-reference deal timing against phase transitions for a partnership velocity metric no competitor tool offers. The infrastructure is there. The ceiling is narrative, not technical.

Note: Peer reviews unanimously identified this as the weakest position — reframes production risks as opportunities without addressing readiness.

Outsider "I can't tell if error handling is real or aspirational"

Everything is a description of what the builder believes they built. The 300-drug cap is a business decision disguised as a technical detail. The major pharma list is a maintenance trap.

"50-60% enrichment" means 40-50% stay empty. The description frames this as a win — nearly half of drugs with no mechanism of action listed is a gap for competitive differentiation. Can't tell if the output is legible to a non-technical pharma analyst.

Executor "The bones are solid. The gaps are in silent failures." — Voted strongest by all 3 reviewers

Execution gaps: (1) 300 hard cap — oncology has 500+ drugs. (2) 20 mechanism enrichment lookups per phase is too low. (3) Major pharma list needs validation. (4) No error surfacing when entity resolution uses fallback strategies.

Monday morning checklist: Run on NSCLC, PD-1, and ADC. Check drug counts vs manual Cortellis web query. Count blank mechanism entries. Audit pharma list against top 20 by revenue. Grep report generator for resolution failure warnings.

Peer Review Highlights

Unanimous strongest: The Executor — concrete, actionable, falsifiable validation steps.

Unanimous weakest: The Expansionist — motivated reasoning, never answers the production readiness question.

All advisors missed:

  • Output consumer fidelity — who reads these reports and do they have context to spot when something is wrong? No confidence signals in the visual output.
  • Output format as adoption blocker — ASCII-only output is a dead end for pharma analysts who export to decks and models. No JSON/CSV alongside narrative.
  • Downstream harm — a BD analyst pasting a truncated landscape into a board deck makes a bad strategic decision with false confidence.
